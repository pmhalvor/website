I'm training a Feed forward network with a few hidden linear layers. Should I be applying an activation function after every layer, or only once after all hidden layers before output layer?

Whether you should apply activation functions after every hidden layer or only once after all hidden layers depends on the specific problem you are trying to solve and the characteristics of your data. Here are a few guidelines to consider:

  1. Non-linearity Requirement: Activation functions introduce non-linearity into the network, allowing it to learn complex patterns in the data. If your problem is non-linear (which is often the case in real-world scenarios), you should apply activation functions after every hidden layer. This enables the network to capture and model intricate relationships within the data.

  2. Depth of the Network: Deeper networks tend to learn more complex representations of the data. Applying activation functions after every hidden layer can help in capturing hierarchical features at different levels of abstraction. However, very deep networks might suffer from issues like vanishing gradients, so techniques like batch normalization and skip connections (residual networks) are often used to mitigate these problems.

  3. Computational Efficiency: Sometimes, applying activation functions after every layer can be computationally expensive, especially if you're dealing with large networks. In such cases, people often use activation functions less frequently to reduce computation costs. However, advancements in hardware and software optimizations have mitigated this concern to some extent.

  4. Vanishing/Exploding Gradients: In deep networks, gradients can vanish or explode during backpropagation, making it difficult to train the network. Activation functions like ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) mitigate the vanishing gradient problem to some extent, but it's still a concern. Proper initialization techniques and careful selection of activation functions can help manage this issue.

  5. Specific Architectures: Certain network architectures, like recurrent neural networks (RNNs), often use specific activation functions tailored to their requirements. For example, the hyperbolic tangent (tanh) activation function is commonly used in RNNs due to its range between -1 and 1, which helps in preventing exploding gradients.

In summary, if your problem is non-linear, it's generally a good practice to apply activation functions after every hidden layer. Common choices include ReLU, Leaky ReLU, or variants like Parametric ReLU (PReLU) depending on the specific problem and characteristics of your data. However, always be open to experimenting and fine-tuning based on the performance of your network on validation data.

What is weigh decay on my optimizer and how should I tune it?

Weight decay, also known as L2 regularization, is a technique used to prevent overfitting in machine learning models, including neural networks. It works by adding a penalty term to the loss function during training, discouraging the model from learning overly complex patterns that might fit the training data too closely. In the context of neural networks, weight decay modifies the optimization objective by adding a term to the loss function proportional to the sum of squares of the model's parameters (weights). This modification encourages the model to prefer smaller weights, effectively reducing the complexity of the model.

  1. Start with a small value: Begin with a small value for λ\lambdaλ, such as 10−610^{-6}10−6, and observe the model's performance on a validation dataset. Training the model with a small weight decay helps prevent overfitting but might not be sufficient in some cases.

  5. Monitor both training and validation performance: Keep an eye on both training and validation performance metrics.
