Introduction¶

This note will explain how to analyze a document based on the frequencies distributions of the document's vocabulary. Such word frequency distributions can give interesting insights about the document, answering questions like "Are there implicit biases within the text?" and "Is this text actually written by the specified author?"

For simplicity, this note will only scratch the surface of finding such distributions, avoiding some of the heavy analysis (let's leave that to the experts).

For our data, we will use the Brown Corpus which comes with installation of nltk. Curated in 1961, this corpus was the first of it's kind and "set the bar for the scientific study of the frequency and distribution of word categories in everyday language use". This historical context is important to keep in mind when analyzing specific attributes of the corpus, as historical texts often carry with them the biases of the cultures they were produced in. More details on the Brown Corpus and be found at it's wiki page.

Pronouns¶

Our experiment will look into the numbers of masculine pronouns (he, him) versus feminine pronouns (she, her) found in the Brown Corpus. The words of the corpus are found using the nltk.corpus.brown.words() method. We use the nltk.FreqDist() to quickly count the occurrences of our 4 pronouns:

In [4]:
pronouns = ['he', 'him', 'she', 'her']
In [5]:
brown_freq = nltk.FreqDist(
    [w for w in brown.words() if w in pronouns]
)

Out[5]:

We already see an imbalance between the representation of the two pronoun types. Remember, this corpus includes only texts produced before 1961. Therefore, this misrepresentation merely reflects the societal views of the two genders at the time of production.

It could still be interesting to see how this imbalance changes between the different genres labeled in the Brown corpus. For now, we will look specifically at: news, religion, government, fiction, romance.

Let's now group these results based on the pronouns' respective gender identity. This process can be hard coded as a nested for loop, iterating through the genres and pronouns in the cfd variable created above.

def group_by_gender(cfd=cfd):
    grouped = {}
    for g in cfd.keys():
        m = 0
        f = 0
        for p in cfd[g].keys():
            d = {}

            if p in ['he', 'him']:
                m += cfd[g][p]
            else:
                f += cfd[g][p]
        grouped[g] = {
            'masculine': m,
            'feminine': f
        }
    return grouped

grouped_cdf = group_by_gender(cfd)

Pronoun forms¶

We continue now by analyzing the occurrences of the different forms in which the gender pronouns take on.

Formally speaking, we want to see if the relative frequency of the objective form, her, within the feminine personal pronouns is higher than the relative frequency of the objective form, him, within the masculine personal pronouns.

This will give us an idea of how the gender pronouns are used in English.

From the plot above, it seems that the initial hypothesis was in fact correct. The feminine objective pronoun her is used more frequently than the nominative she, whereas the opposite can be observed for the masculine objective and nominative pronouns, him and he respectively.

The two tables explain this scenario from two different points of view. The first table says that when looking at all the nominative pronouns in the text, 77% are masculine and 23% are feminine, whereas the objective pronouns are balanced between the two genders. The second table shows that out of all the masculine pronouns used, 72% are nominative and 28% are objective. When looking at the feminine pronouns, the split between nominative and objective is closer to 40%-60%, respectively. This tells us that the English language (per 1961) is heavily diluted with the masculine, nominative pronoun he.

This doesn't tell us the whole truth though, since her is the feminine equivalent of both his and him. In other words, our specific selection of pronouns is very strict, and fails capture all lexemme of the gender pronouns, arbitrarily.

To take this into account, we could expand our search for occurrences of 'his'+'him' versus those of 'her'+'hers'. This way we could count all of the objective forms for both genders, instead of the non-possessive. This exercise can be left up to the reader.

Possessive pronouns¶

Another way of separating the two objective forms is to use a tagged corpus, which separates between the two forms of her, i.e, if the corpus tags her as a personal pronoun differently from her as a possessive pronoun. The tagged Brown corpus with the full tag set does that.

We will use this to count the occurrences of she, he, her, him as personal pronouns and her, his, hers as possessive pronouns.

Conclusion¶

In the first part of this exercise, we found that masculine pronouns were a lot more common than feminine pronouns, across almost all genres. This reflects women's historical place in society and helps illustrate that language can carry with it the biases of previous generations.

In reference to the use of nominative pronouns versus objective pronouns across the two genders, it first seemed as though feminine pronouns were more likely to be objective than masculine. However, as was shown through further analysis, this great difference was likely due to the distinction between possessive and objective personal pronouns for the masculine pronouns ‘his’ and ‘him’, whereas feminine pronouns of these two forms were grouped together as ‘her’.

When working with language technologies, these biases must be taken into consideration, so as to not build predictive models where certain groups can be discriminated against. It’s important to note that the Brown Corpus was the first representation of the English language as a whole, per 1961. This was a historically different time than where society is today, especially when taking into consideration the Civil Rights movement of the 60’s and 70’s, as well as antidiscrimination efforts and laws that have grown drastically in popularity within the past few decades.

Language technologists must make sure to be using as up to date representations of the languages they modeling to both correctly represent the language, and to follow suite with the societal changes inflicted upon the language.
